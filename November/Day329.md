My dear reader, we are invited to contemplate a sequence of five mysteries, each a window through which a peculiar and strengthening light falls upon our own path. The first is that moment at the Jordan, where He who knew no sin submitted to a baptism of repentance, and the voice of the Father was heard from the opened heavens, declaring Him the Beloved. Then we come to the wedding at Cana, a quiet affair in a small town, where at His Mother's gentle urging, the water of common necessity was made the wine of joy, the first public sign of a glory that transforms things from within.

The third mystery finds Him walking the dusty roads, proclaiming a Kingdom that is both at hand and yet to come, calling every soul to a great turning about, a metanoia, from the shadowed paths toward the light. Then, upon the high mountain, He was transfigured before His friends, His face shining like the sun and His clothes become as light itself, while the Law and the Prophets, in the figures of Moses and Elijah, bore witness to Him.

And finally, in the upper room, He performed the most profound of all transformations. Taking the simple bread and wine of the earth, He made them into the very substance of His own Life—His Body and Blood—a perpetual gift and sacrifice, the true sustenance for our pilgrimage, and the foundation of that priesthood which would serve this great mystery until the end of days.
